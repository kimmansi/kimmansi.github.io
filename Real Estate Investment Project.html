DIVINE Real Estate Investments Project

My website creation process.

All my steps from moodboarding to logo creation. Then wireframing to prototyping. And then coding the website.

The moodboard. This client's colors were fuscia pink and black from the beginning as they were already used heavily in their brand. They also had strong meaning and imagery behind the choice of those colors. So moodboarding was more about finding what other images not only fit their color branding but also gave us some other words to associate with their mission. I found a sign that had "grit" used as an acronym that I liked. But I also liked the colors the artist used. There was a faded purple gray color that I thought might be a good middle hue color to ground the hot pink but also soften the black power color. I also researched the local market and their competitors to see what colors and imagery they presented. In a sea of blue, green, and yellow, the pink and black would definitely stand out. But I had to make sure they presented as much solid, trustworthy, and dependable as much as they yelled dynamic and differnt. I also started looking at block type fonts as I knew I wanted the DIVINE part of the name all in caps. And I originally thought to change the feel of "investments" by using a script since the company was trying to attract clients that otherwise might find an investment company hard to approach.



The logo. I did three iterations of this logo. First playing with background colors. Then narrowing down imagery. And then finally deciding that the pink needed two solid but complimentary block fonts to balance instead of using a script one. I did custom graphics for all the buildings. And I chose to highlight homes and buildings because the company while offering other products, most definitely concentrated on real estate as a means for people to grow wealth. I did the house design with the empty home progressing to the home full of lines to represent the growth of the company and also the of the client's portfolio if they signed up with DI. I also did a skyline design with a gradual increasing height of buildings with the solid square of strength representing DI in the middle to signify the growth they could bring to a portfolio. And the two windows, one dark and other light and higher up to also represent that as well. The pink triangle was also an element that important to the original brand concept and mission statement of the company. I still kept it but this time rather than a logo shape border element, I used it to bring attention to the Divine name and also as the entry point to the line element building drawings.



The sitemap. This was a complicated sitemap to plan out. The product page would have several ways to search several different products. So pages linking to pages, linking back to pages. I also had to decide if we were going to include the outside functions and social media workshops on the site as well. I did decide that the community event offerings and education opportunities were inherent to their brand, so they did belong on the website.



The wireframe sketches. There was going to be a lot of elements to these pages. A lot of custom graphics, info graphics, and big search elements along with a need for picture galleries for all the properties. So I spent time really trying to get an idea of flow.





The low fidelity wireframes.Everything I planned out in sketches translated well to lo-fi wireframes in Figma. Just played a little with button size to refine how they would compare to other elements. Also added in some different gray fill in colors to help start making final decisions as to how all the different custom graphics would play together. I ultimately decided to go with the eggplant purple as my primary background color.



The prototype. The prototype came out remarkably like my original wireframe sketches without too many changes. I mainly found that I had to adapt the main page and about me page most. Surprising to me the product page, which was the most complicated out of them all, stayed pretty true to the original sketch.




Is it responsive? Due to all the custom graphics, I struggled a bit to get everything responsive at all device widths.









The final website. I am most proud of the JavaScript tab search function that I was able to design and implement for same page tab cycling through the various products available.
